## The point of 'having a purpose' 

I have been trying to get my mentees to define a 'purpose' for themselves recently. The goal is to help them succeed and get ahead in their career. I want them to have a vision of what they want, so I can help them align their career and progress with their vision.

But do people really need a purpose? It's really hard to find a purpose if you're young? And what do you even do when you have a purpose? Can you change your 'purpose'? What's the point of a purpose if it's changing all the time?

What to do once you have a purpose but you don't have control over your life, and no realistic way to fulfill that purpose?

Let's start with, do young people even need a purpose? Is that a pointless exercise? Perhaps people in their late 20s and early 30s would do better with a purpose because they understand themselves better and they know what they want better. Does it make sense to ask a 22 or 23 year old to come up with a purpose for their life?

It helps to verbalize and externalize the immediate goals and wants, because on a day-to-day basis your priorities are changing regularly. Your projects are changing. Your personal relationships are involved. With so much confusion, you tend to forget to anchor yourself in something you care about. Often, the only reason for not coming back to what you care about is simply not remembering it. One might argue if you really cared about something you would remember, but the issue is not of motivation but of human fallibility and technology. A lack of ability to hold consistent long-term goals.

A clearly defined purpose that is shared with others is a technology, a spiritual technology, to align yourself into more longer term projects. Framed that way, the question, “Does it matter if you have a purpose if your purpose keeps on changing?” makes more sense. Yes, it does. It makes sense to have a purpose, even if it keeps on changing, because your future direction can be decided much faster, easier, and clearly if you had a good idea of where you were currently intending on going. You can maybe find a shortcut to your future direction. You can maybe only slightly change your trajectory instead of running around all confused towards your new direction. So, yeah, even for young people, it does make sense.

The other question I raised was, if you have very little to no control over your day-to-day activities, does having a purpose—your long-term goal—even make sense? Is it a waste of time and emotional energy?

It's not a waste of emotional energy, and it still matters. Because it still anchors you and grounds your everyday actions. So that if ever you find yourself at the right time at the right place, you are a lot more prepared to take action on it. You are even likelier to be closer to the final goal if you keep reminding yourself where you want to be, even if you don't eventually end up reaching your final goal. So, yeah, you might not be able to get to the moon, but maybe reminding yourself often that your goal is your purpose to reach the moon will help you. It may help you to be an architect or become a mountain climber, or perhaps an animator or an artist. That is what grounds you and reminds you. Instead of rediscovering the passion over and over after getting distracted, a purpose helps you ground it in the long term. It's always your friend, even if you otherwise have forgotten it.

What I think we need to start with is some very basic rules. You can define your purpose however you want. The easiest way to ask is by asking people what they value in life the most. What is something that you really, really care about and wish to improve, or dedicate yourself to improving or working on? If it's just for yourself, if it's to make a lot of money, that's fine; it's a purpose. It's a simple, clear purpose, so probably a lot easier to identify and achieve.

Sometimes, other purposes are less clear. And there can be conflicting purposes. Maybe you want an awesome career, but you also want to take care of your family. But your career won't let you do that, but you still want an awesome career and don't want to compromise. In that case, you don't have to make a choice, or you have to find something that collaborates on both. And you can combine them both. Thinking explicitly about things instead of just going off vibes is better because you know how to think about it better.
