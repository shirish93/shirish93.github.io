Novel Craft Course: Dictation and Transcription Workshop

Module 1: Establishing Trust and Core Themes

Essay 1: Establishing Authority

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
1.1: The Heart Method Dictation Activity: Dictate a scene where the narrator reveals an immediate vulnerability, failure, or weakness about the protagonist or the narrator themselves. Focus on frankness and honesty. Establishes emotional authority. Honesty and frankness is one of the two most effective ways to establish authority. Honesty proves to the reader the story will not be about proving the narrator's glory.
1.2: The Head Method Dictation Activity: Dictate a 5-minute technical explanation related to the novel’s world. The narrator must demonstrate deep, researched knowledge to prove competence. Establishes intellectual authority. The second method is demonstrating knowledge. The narrator must be the most-qualified person to tell this story.

Essay 2: Minimalism (Themes and Collage)

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
2.1: Limiting the "Horses" Activity: Identify one major theme or metaphor ("Horse"). Dictate a 10-minute scene. Every image, object, or piece of dialogue must support only that single, simple theme ("basic melody"). Builds story depth by keeping only a limited number of themes. Minimalism is about using a limited number of themes, or "Horses," so you are able to build the depth of the story. The structure starts with a simple melody that builds and varies.
2.2: Dictated Collage/Quilting Activity: Dictate two unrelated short anecdotes. They must share the "Horse" theme from L2.1, making them "squares" in a narrative "Quilt". Uses the "Collage" or "Quilt" structure to imply plot or time passing in the fictional world. Whole books can be written as a collage. The collage chapters are best used to slow the plot or imply time passing in your fictional world.

Module 2: Sensory Immersion and Vocal Performance

Essay 3: Using ‘On-the-Body’ Physical Sensation

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
3.1: Going On-the-Body Activity: Dictate a tense scene. Every few sentences must describe a specific physical sensation related to the character’s body (e.g., soles of feet, pulse, dry mouth). Engages the reader physically. This technique is called "going-on-the-body" to demonstrate physical sensation in a story. Any physical sensation can evoke a sympathetic physical response from the reader.
3.2: Violence and Internalization Activity: Dictate a scene with intense conflict. Use only external action and physical details. The goal is to make the story occur in the reader's "mind, heart and gut". Uses intense physical description instead of abstract emotion. You should engage the reader on a physical level to create a reality that can eclipse their actual reality. The goal is to make the story occur in the reader's mind, heart, and gut.

Essay 4: Submerging the "I"

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
4.1: The Monologue Practice Activity: Dictate a scene in the first person. Deliver it as if it were a charming monologue to a friend. Do not focus on the narrator (the "I") as the subject. Uses the first-person voice, but successfully hides the "I". When we see the letter "I" on the page, we recoil because a self-absorbed person irritates us. The answer is to use the first person voice, but hide the I.
4.2: Vocal and Written Audit Activity: Read the transcribed monologue aloud. Stop when the work "clunks". Revise the language based on what the voice finds awkward. Ensures the story maintains the charm and honesty of a monologue and that the writing flows well when spoken. This is an important part of Tom Spanbauer’s workshop: reading your work out loud. It should read with the honesty and charm of a monologue.

Module 3: Structural Planning and Pacing

Essay 5 & 24: Nuts and Bolts: Hiding a Gun / Tell a Lie, Bury a Gun

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
5.1: The Buried Prop Dictation Activity: Dictate the opening of a scene. Introduce a small, mundane detail or object (the "gun"). Acknowledge it, then drop it immediately. The object functions as a threat or promise that will lead to plot resolution later. Hiding a gun is a form of device where you introduce a detail, forget it, and then re-introduce it to bring plot to resolution. The buried gun is a promise or a threat you fulfill to wrap up your story.
5.2: The Corroding Lie Activity: Dictate an intermediate scene where the protagonist tells a crucial lie. Focus the dictation on the character’s physical gestures while lying. The lie acts as a type of buried gun; its eventual reveal will bring justice or resolution. The Lie is a crime committed before the story starts, but it corrodes the guilty killers until they are revealed and brought to justice.

Essay 18: Killing Time – Part One

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
6.1: Condensing Narrative Time Activity: Dictate a sequence that covers three years of time in under three minutes of dictation. Use gradual reveals to imply time has passed. Controls pacing and speeds momentum by condensing long periods of time. One consistent problem is that most student work is plotting or pacing too slow. You need methods for implying that time has passed in the narrative.
6.2: The Gradual Reveal (Tension) Activity: Dictate a high-tension scene. Hold back the key piece of information, letting tension build through a gradual series of smaller reveals. Creates tension by slowly delivering information, allowing the reader to appreciate each step. Good plotting needs the secret of the story to be revealed in a gradual, teasing way. The process of accumulating nakedness (reveals) builds tension.

Module 4: Advanced Structure and Intent

Essay 12 & 13: Talking Shapes: The Quilt / The Thumbnail

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
7.1: The Thumbnail Overture Activity: Dictate the first chapter of the novel using the 'Thumbnail' structure. This must be a contained, limited physical scene in the present. While dictating the scene, insert brief, veiled voiceovers or references that clearly hint at the full trajectory of the plot or the ending. Creates tension by assuring the reader of full knowledge and manages expectations. The 'thumbnail' opening chapter offers teasing glimpses of the entire plot. This technique lessens tension about the resolution, but creates more tension by assuring the reader time will not be wasted.
7.2: Structuring the Quilt Activity: For the next three chapters, dictate three seemingly separate short stories or self-contained anecdotes (the "squares"). Ensure these stories share a common thematic "envelope". Creates a novel structure using quilted short stories. The "Quilt" shape is a novel that provides a context ("envelope") for telling many short stories, like in Generation X and Haunted. The shorter stories are the "squares".

Module 5: Refining Language (Clarity and Detail)

Essay 6: Beware the "Thesis Statement"

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
8.1: Detail Accumulation Dictation Activity: Select a broad, vague statement about your character (e.g., "She was having a terrible day"). Dictate a short scene that intentionally avoids saying that general statement, but instead unpacks the details. Replaces vague generalities with strong, specific details. Blanket statements like "Robert woke up, hating his life" are called "Thesis Statements". This is about unpacking the details to make the fiction work better.
8.2: Fixing Weak Openings Activity: Review the transcribed text. Identify weak opening paragraphs. Dictate a stronger, detailed second paragraph to replace the generalization. Strengthens weak opening paragraphs by prioritizing detail over general summary. If the opening is slow or fails to grab attention, look out for a Thesis Sentence. You can bring the strong detail that should have been in your opening to the beginning of the story.

Essay 11: Learning from the Cliches... Then Leaving Them Behind

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
9.1: Unpacking the Physical Cliché Activity: Select a physical cliché (e.g., "shaking like a leaf," "wringing my hands"). Dictate a short scene where you use the cliché as a shorthand shortcut, then immediately dictate three or four unique, specific sensory details. Translates lazy clichés into specific, sensory physical details. A cliché functions as a shorthand shortcut for something real and physical. You should unpack and translate the entire physical moment into a series of sensory details.
9.2: Avoiding Ruts (Voice) Activity: Dictate a new scene intentionally avoiding the phrases or predictable patterns ("ruts") you rely on. Focus on using different methods or vocabulary to create your own voice. Practices creating original voice and resisting predictable writing patterns. Read work and realize how you think in the same patterns as the author you’re aping. You must develop your own method or tool to use in creating your own voice.

Module 6: Enhancing Dialogue and Character Identity

Essay 9 & 19: Punctuation with Gesture / Disconnected Dialogue

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
10.1: Dictated Gesture Attribution Activity: Dictate a conversation (Dialogue A). Replace all standard "he/she said" attributions with physical actions, gesture, or sensation that reflects the subtext of the line. Uses physical business/gesture to control dialogue timing and add visual tension. Replacing "he said/she said" markers with physical action allows the gesture and attribution to control timing better than standard punctuation. Physical business adds tension and visual interest.
10.2: Questions and Power Dialogue Activity: Dictate a conversation (Dialogue B) where characters primarily respond to each other with questions rather than clear answers, or fail to respond to the prompt they were given. Uses ambiguity and "disconnected dialogue" to build tension and imply issues not spoken aloud. Good plotting is about playing with power: a character gains power, loses it, regains it. The first method for disconnection is to consider Questions instead of answers.

Essay 22: Names Versus Pronouns

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
11.1: Expanding Identity Labels Activity: Dictate a scene focused on one major character. Avoid third-person pronouns ("he/she/it"). Refer to the character using varied labels, nicknames, or physical descriptions. Creates a maximum amount of tension and reinforces identity shifts. The goal is to avoid third-person pronouns. The problem with third-person pronouns is they cause repeating nouns to become monotonous.
11.2: Voice and Stereotype Activity: Introduce three secondary characters. Give each a quick, descriptive label (e.g., "Miss Turner"). Dictate dialogue and action that reinforces that label. Creates consistent characteristics using titles or nicknames based on appearance or relationship. Before using proper names, consider stronger labels based on appearance or relationship, such as titles or nicknames. You should refer to people by their varied, evolving names.

Module 7: Objects, Observation, and Flow

Essay 20: Objects

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
12.1: Dictating the Object Inventory Activity: Dictate a description of three important objects (like a family photo). For each, dictate how it relates to the character's eventual salvation or downfall. Ensures key objects are "loaded with meaning" and serve a plot function. Most main characters will have one important object that represents their eventual salvation or downfall. Objects can function as a Memory Cue, Buried Gun, or Gesture Prop.
12.2: Object Morphing Dictation Activity: Dictate a sequence where one object is used in three separate scenes. Dictate how the object’s symbolic meaning morphs or grows with each scene. Builds tension and momentum by having limited objects fulfill multiple functions. Limiting objects and recycling them throughout a story gives them more power. Look for ways to morph your objects from appearance to appearance.

Essay 10: When You Can’t Find a Writing Workshop

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
13.1: Finding the "Trap" Setting Activity: Designate a specific, quiet, distraction-free location ("trap"). Dictate for 30 minutes only in that location, focusing on controlled discipline. Utilizes a distraction-minimal setting to write more. You should find the boring setting that will "trap" you on a regular basis until you’re done. Find a setting where distractions are minimal, and everything is controlled except your imagination.
13.2: Observation and Memory Cues Activity: Go to a public place. Dictate descriptions of real people telling stories, paying attention to the "memory cues" (objects or sensory details) that trigger those stories. Gathers real-world material and practices observing how people talk and "exhaust their emotions". Look for places where people tell stories and for the "memory cues" that trigger those stories. Most people are dying to talk, tell their stories, and exhaust their emotions about the past.

Essay 7/8: Reading Out Loud – Part One & Two

Lesson Dictation Focus & Activity Craft Outcome Source Citation
14.1: The Vocal Revision Activity: Read the transcribed version of your latest scene aloud. When you "hear every bad decision" or clunky phrase, stop. Use dictation to re-voice the sentence focusing on correct timing and pace. Uses the aural feedback loop to correct clunky phrases and pacing issues. Reading out loud is the only way to know you have the correct timing and pace. When reading out loud, you hear every bad decision you’ve made in your work.
14.2: Embracing the Storyteller Voice Activity: Dictate narrative details, consciously using the "little voice" that you rely on when talking to someone. Embrace this voice as the core of your storytelling. Strengthens the conversational flow. You are doomed to the chattering "Monkey Mind," that little voice that tells you you're bad or fat. Accepting that little voice is the only escape from it, helping to build your skill as a storyteller.
